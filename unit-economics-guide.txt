SENSA WELLNESS
Unit Economics Guide
Last updated: May 2026

---

INTRODUCTION

Unit economics answer one fundamental question: does Sensa make money on each
customer, and how much? Before worrying about scale, you need to know whether
the underlying business is viable at the individual transaction level. A company
can grow fast and still destroy value if its unit economics are broken. This
guide walks through every section of the unit economics template and explains
what to fill in, how to calculate it, and what the numbers mean.

---

SECTION 1: PRODUCT PRICING

What to fill in:
Enter the selling price for each product tier (1-Test Kit, 3-Test Pack,
4-Test Pack) and how many of each you sell or expect to sell per month.

Why it matters:
Your pricing is the ceiling on everything else. Every cost, margin, and ratio
flows from what a customer actually pays. The spreadsheet will calculate your
monthly revenue and your Average Order Value (AOV), which is the weighted
average of what a customer spends per transaction across all product types.

Key questions to ask yourself:
- Are your prices based on what competitors charge, what customers will pay,
  or what you need to cover your costs? Ideally all three.
- Does your pricing encourage customers to buy the larger packs? A customer
  who buys a 4-Test Pack is worth more upfront and is more likely to build a
  testing habit.
- Is there room to raise prices? Consumer health products often have more
  pricing power than founders assume, especially when backed by science.

Target: No specific target here, but your price must be high enough to
support a gross margin of at least 60% after COGS.

---

SECTION 2: COST OF GOODS SOLD (COGS)

What to fill in:
The direct cost of producing one test. This includes every physical component
that goes into the kit.

Components:

CRP Test Strip
This is your most important cost to get right. The lateral flow assay strip is
the core of the product. At low volumes (under 1,000 units) you may pay $1.50
to $3.00 per strip. At 10,000+ units, you can likely negotiate $0.50 to $1.00.
Get quotes from at least three suppliers. Ask for price break schedules at 1k,
5k, 10k, and 50k units so you can model future COGS.

Lancet
A commodity item. At any reasonable volume, this should cost $0.05 to $0.15
per unit. Do not over-index on this. It is unlikely to move your margins
meaningfully, but it is worth bundling into a supplier order if possible.

Alcohol Wipe
Also a commodity. Budget $0.02 to $0.05 per unit.

Packaging
This includes the box, insert card, and printed instructions. Packaging has a
larger impact on perceived quality than most founders realize. Budget $0.50 to
$2.00 per unit depending on material quality. At early volumes, packaging is
often the second-largest cost after the strip. As volume grows, unit costs drop.

Kitting and Assembly
Someone has to put the components together and seal the box. This is typically
done by a contract manufacturer or a third-party logistics provider (3PL).
Budget $0.50 to $2.00 per unit. At low volumes this feels expensive. At scale
it becomes negligible.

Why it matters:
COGS is the first number subtracted from your revenue. Every dollar you reduce
COGS by (through volume discounts, supplier negotiations, or design changes)
falls directly to gross profit. Getting your COGS right is the foundation of
everything else.

Target: Total COGS should be less than 40% of your selling price, leaving at
least a 60% gross margin.

---

SECTION 3: VARIABLE COSTS PER ORDER

What to fill in:
Costs that occur with every order but are not part of the physical product.

Outbound Shipping
This is often larger than founders expect. Shipping a small box via USPS
First Class or UPS Ground within the US typically costs $4 to $8. If you are
self-fulfilling, you can negotiate commercial rates with USPS or UPS. If you
use a 3PL, shipping is often bundled with pick-and-pack fees. Consider whether
you offer free shipping (common in consumer e-commerce) and if so, this cost
must be absorbed into your pricing.

Payment Processing
Stripe charges 2.9% of the transaction plus $0.30 per charge. On a $30 order
that is about $1.17. On a $60 order it is about $2.04. This is non-negotiable
at early stage but can be renegotiated with Stripe at significant volume.

Returns and Replacements
Not every order goes perfectly. Budget a small percentage (1% to 3%) of
orders for replacements due to defective strips, shipping damage, or customer
dissatisfaction. Sensa's current policy offers free replacements for defective
kits, which is the right call for customer trust but must be accounted for
in the economics.

Why it matters:
Variable costs are what you subtract from gross profit to get contribution
margin. Contribution margin is the amount each order actually contributes
toward covering your fixed costs and generating profit. It is the more honest
measure of unit profitability than gross margin alone.

Target: Contribution margin should be at least 40% of AOV.

---

SECTION 4: UNIT ECONOMICS (CALCULATED)

These are the outputs of Sections 1 through 3. Once you fill in the inputs,
these numbers will tell you whether the business model works.

Gross Profit per Test
Revenue per test minus COGS per test. This tells you how much you make before
shipping and other variable costs.

Gross Margin %
Gross profit divided by revenue, expressed as a percentage. For a consumer
health product like Sensa, you should target 60% or higher. If your gross
margin is under 50%, your pricing is too low or your COGS is too high.

Contribution Margin per Order
AOV minus COGS minus variable costs. This is the most important number on the
sheet. It tells you how much money each order actually generates that can be
used to cover marketing, salaries, software, and overhead.

Contribution Margin %
Contribution margin divided by AOV. Target 40% or higher.

---

SECTION 5: CUSTOMER ACQUISITION

What to fill in:
Your monthly marketing spend and how many new customers it produces.

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
CAC = Total Marketing Spend / Number of New Customers Acquired

Example: If you spend $2,000 on Instagram ads in a month and get 40 new
customers, your CAC is $50.

CAC is one of the most important numbers in the business. It determines how
much you need to earn from each customer to be profitable. A CAC that exceeds
your contribution margin on a first purchase is fine IF customers reorder.
A CAC that exceeds your total LTV is a business that is losing money on every
customer.

What drives CAC:
- Ad spend efficiency (cost per click, click-through rate, conversion rate)
- Organic channels (SEO, content, word of mouth) reduce blended CAC
- Referral programs are one of the most cost-effective ways to lower CAC
  in consumer health

Target: CAC should be no more than one-third of your Contribution LTV
(LTV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or better).

---

SECTION 6: LIFETIME VALUE (LTV)

What to fill in:
Your estimate of how often a customer reorders and how long they stay active.

LTV is where Sensa's business model has the most leverage. A customer who
tests once is worth their one-time contribution margin. A customer who tests
monthly for two years is worth dramatically more. The habit of monthly CRP
tracking is the core value driver.

Gross LTV
Average revenue per customer per year multiplied by estimated lifespan in
years. This is the top-line number but does not account for costs.

Contribution LTV
Average contribution margin per order multiplied by number of orders over a
customer's lifetime. This is the number you compare to CAC, because CAC is
paid up front to acquire a customer whose profits arrive over time.

Example:
- Customer buys a 3-Test Pack every 3 months ($45 AOV, 4x per year)
- Contribution margin per order: $20
- Average customer lifespan: 2 years
- Contribution LTV: $20 x 4 orders/year x 2 years = $160
- If CAC is $50, LTV:CAC = 3.2x (healthy)

Key insight:
Even a modest improvement in reorder rate dramatically improves LTV. If you
can move a customer from testing twice a year to four times a year, you double
their LTV without spending an additional dollar on acquisition. This is why
the Sensa app, streaks, and push notifications matter. They are not just
product features. They are retention mechanics that directly impact LTV.

---

SECTION 7: KEY RATIOS

LTV:CAC Ratio
Contribution LTV divided by CAC. This is the single most important ratio for
a subscription or repeat-purchase business.

  Under 1:1 — You are losing money on every customer. Not sustainable.
  1:1 to 2:1 — Marginal. You are covering CAC but not generating much profit.
  3:1 — The standard benchmark for a healthy consumer business.
  5:1 or higher — Strong unit economics. You may be under-investing in growth.

Payback Period
How many months until the contribution margin from a customer covers the CAC
you paid to acquire them.

Formula: CAC / (Monthly Contribution Margin per customer)

Example: CAC of $50, customer generates $10/month in contribution margin.
Payback period = 5 months.

Target: Under 12 months for a consumer product. Shorter is better because
it means you get your acquisition investment back faster and can reinvest it.

MRR and ARR
Monthly and Annual Recurring Revenue give you a high-level view of the
business trajectory. Even if Sensa is not technically a subscription, if
customers reorder predictably, you can model a quasi-subscription MRR.

---

SECTION 8: BREAK-EVEN ANALYSIS

What to fill in:
Your total monthly fixed costs. These are costs you pay regardless of how
many units you sell: salaries, software subscriptions, office space, legal,
accounting, etc.

Break-even Units
Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin per Order

This tells you exactly how many orders you need per month to cover all your
fixed costs. Every order above break-even is profit.

Example:
- Fixed costs: $10,000/month
- Contribution margin per order: $25
- Break-even: 400 orders/month

This number should feel concrete and achievable. If break-even requires 5,000
orders/month before you can generate profit, your fixed cost structure is too
heavy for your current stage.

---

SECTION 9: SCENARIO PLANNING

What to fill in:
Three versions of the business: conservative, base case, and optimistic.

This section forces you to think about the range of outcomes and what drives
the difference between them. The key variables to flex are:

- Monthly order volume (driven by marketing and organic growth)
- AOV (driven by product mix and pricing)
- COGS per test (driven by supplier negotiations and volume discounts)
- CAC (driven by marketing efficiency and organic/referral growth)

Use this section to answer questions like:
- What does the business look like if we hit 500 orders/month vs. 2,000?
- How much does our LTV:CAC improve if we negotiate COGS down by 30%?
- At what order volume does the business become profitable?

NEXT STEPS

1. Get real supplier quotes for CRP strips. This is the most uncertain input
   and the one that most affects your COGS. Contact at least three suppliers
   with your specs and ask for price breaks at 1k, 5k, and 10k units.

2. Calculate your actual CAC from any marketing spend to date. Even a small
   data set gives you a starting point.

3. Survey early users about reorder intent. Ask: "How often do you plan to
   test?" This gives you an LTV estimate grounded in real customer feedback.

4. Fill in the spreadsheet with real numbers, even if some are estimates.
   A model with rough estimates is more useful than a perfect model you have
   not built yet.

5. Revisit this model monthly as you get more data. Unit economics improve
   as you grow (volume discounts, better CAC from organic growth, higher LTV
   from better retention). Tracking this over time shows you whether the
   business is moving in the right direction.

GLOSSARY

AOV — Average Order Value. The average amount a customer spends per transaction.
CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost. Total marketing spend divided by new customers acquired.
COGS — Cost of Goods Sold. The direct cost of producing one unit.
Contribution Margin — Revenue minus COGS minus variable costs per order.
Gross Margin — Gross profit (revenue minus COGS) divided by revenue.
LTV — Lifetime Value. Total contribution margin generated by one customer over their relationship with Sensa.
MRR — Monthly Recurring Revenue.
ARR — Annual Recurring Revenue. MRR multiplied by 12.
Payback Period — Months required for a customer's contribution margin to cover their CAC.
3PL — Third-party logistics provider. A company that handles warehousing, kitting, and fulfillment.
