How Do You Reduce Inflammation in Your Stomach?

Inflammation of the stomach lining, known as gastritis, is usually caused by a specific, identifiable trigger. That means the most effective way to reduce it is to find and address the cause rather than chase generic anti-inflammatory tips.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

You reduce inflammation in the stomach by treating its cause. The most common cause of stomach inflammation (gastritis) is infection with the bacterium Helicobacter pylori, which requires antibiotics from a doctor. Other frequent causes are regular use of NSAID pain relievers (like aspirin and ibuprofen) and heavy alcohol use, both of which can be eased by stopping the offending substance. Supportive steps include avoiding alcohol and irritating foods, eating smaller meals, not smoking, and managing stress. Because gastritis often needs medical diagnosis and treatment, persistent stomach pain should be evaluated by a healthcare provider rather than self-treated.

Stomach inflammation is different from the general, whole-body inflammation that diet and exercise influence. Gastritis is a localized condition with specific, well-understood causes, and that is actually good news, because it means the treatment is usually targeted rather than vague. The catch is that the leading cause, an H. pylori infection, needs medical testing and antibiotics to clear, so self-care alone is often not enough. This article explains the common triggers and the supportive steps that help, but persistent or severe symptoms belong in a doctor's office.

What Causes Inflammation in the Stomach?

The most common cause of stomach inflammation is infection with Helicobacter pylori, a bacterium that colonizes the stomach lining and provokes chronic inflammation. The next most common causes are frequent use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as aspirin and ibuprofen, which is one of the most common causes of acute gastritis, and heavy alcohol use, which irritates and erodes the stomach lining. Less common causes include autoimmune gastritis, in which the immune system attacks the stomach lining, severe stress from major illness or surgery, and bile reflux. Identifying which of these applies is the key to reducing the inflammation, which is why medical evaluation matters.

How Do You Treat H. Pylori Gastritis?

H. pylori gastritis is treated with a doctor-prescribed combination of antibiotics, usually taken for 7 to 14 days, often alongside acid-reducing medication. Because H. pylori is a bacterial infection, no diet or supplement clears it; eradication requires the full antibiotic course, and finishing the entire prescription is important to prevent resistance and recurrence. Testing for H. pylori can be done through a breath, stool, or blood test, or during an endoscopy. If you have chronic stomach symptoms, asking your provider whether H. pylori testing is appropriate is a sensible first step, since treating the infection often resolves the inflammation at its source.

How Do NSAIDs and Alcohol Affect the Stomach?

NSAIDs and alcohol both damage the stomach lining and can cause or worsen gastritis. NSAIDs such as aspirin, ibuprofen, and naproxen reduce the protective mucus layer of the stomach and are one of the most common causes of acute gastritis, especially with frequent or high-dose use. Alcohol directly irritates and can erode the stomach lining, and heavy use is a well-recognized trigger. Acute gastritis caused by NSAIDs or alcohol often improves once you stop the offending substance. If you rely on NSAIDs for pain, talk to your provider about alternatives or protective medication rather than simply stopping a prescribed drug on your own.

What Diet and Lifestyle Steps Help Stomach Inflammation?

Supportive diet and lifestyle steps can ease stomach inflammation while the underlying cause is being treated. Helpful measures include avoiding alcohol, limiting foods that trigger your symptoms (for many people spicy, acidic, fried, or highly processed foods), eating smaller and more frequent meals, not smoking, and managing stress. Some people find that gentler, less irritating foods and staying upright after eating help symptoms. These steps support the stomach lining but do not replace treating the cause: if the problem is H. pylori, diet alone will not fix it. How Long Does Gastritis Take to Heal?

Most gastritis improves quickly once the cause is addressed, often within days to a couple of weeks. Acute gastritis from NSAIDs or alcohol frequently settles soon after you stop the offending substance, and symptoms tend to ease for most people with appropriate treatment. When the cause is H. pylori, symptom relief usually follows the antibiotic course, though the stomach lining continues healing after the infection is cleared. Chronic gastritis, which develops slowly, may take longer to resolve and depends on managing the underlying cause. If symptoms persist despite treatment, that is a reason to return to your provider, since ongoing inflammation may need further evaluation. The general rule is encouraging: gastritis is very treatable, and lingering symptoms usually signal that the cause has not yet been fully identified or addressed.

Can Stress Cause Stomach Inflammation?

Severe physiologic stress can cause a form of acute gastritis, though everyday psychological stress plays a smaller and less direct role. Major physical stressors such as serious illness, major surgery, severe burns, or critical injury can trigger what is sometimes called stress gastritis, related to reduced blood flow and other changes in the critically ill. This is different from the ordinary day-to-day stress of a busy life, which is not a primary cause of gastritis but can worsen how symptoms feel and may influence stomach acid and eating patterns. Managing stress is a reasonable supportive step that can help you feel better and support digestion, but it does not substitute for treating an underlying cause like H. pylori. If stomach symptoms are persistent, the priority remains identifying the specific trigger with a healthcare provider.

Is Stomach Inflammation the Same as Acid Reflux?

No, stomach inflammation (gastritis) is not the same as acid reflux, although they can feel similar and sometimes coexist. Gastritis is inflammation of the stomach lining, while acid reflux, or GERD, is the backflow of stomach acid into the esophagus. Both can cause upper-abdominal or chest discomfort, but their causes and treatments differ. Because the symptoms overlap, distinguishing them usually requires a healthcare provider's evaluation. If your discomfort is more of a burning that rises toward the chest, especially after meals or when lying down, reflux may be involved. See our separate guide on GERD and inflammation.

What Foods Are Gentle on an Inflamed Stomach?

When the stomach lining is inflamed, gentler, less irritating foods are usually better tolerated while the underlying cause is treated. Many people find that bland, low-acid, non-spicy foods such as cooked vegetables, oats, bananas, rice, lean proteins, and non-citrus fruits are easier on the stomach, while spicy, fried, highly acidic, and heavily processed foods tend to aggravate symptoms. Smaller, more frequent meals put less demand on the stomach than large ones, and avoiding alcohol and reducing caffeine can help. Individual tolerance varies, so it is worth noticing which specific foods trigger your symptoms and adjusting accordingly. Keep in mind that these dietary choices are about comfort and supporting the lining, not curing the condition: if the cause is H. pylori, the infection still needs antibiotic treatment regardless of how carefully you eat.

When Should You See a Doctor?

You should see a doctor for stomach inflammation that is persistent, severe, or accompanied by warning signs. Seek prompt medical care for stomach pain that does not improve, vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds, black or tarry stools, unexplained weight loss, or difficulty swallowing, as these can signal bleeding or a more serious condition. Even without alarm symptoms, ongoing stomach discomfort warrants evaluation, because identifying the cause, especially H. pylori, is what allows effective treatment. Gastritis is very treatable, and most cases improve quickly once the cause is addressed. This article is general wellness information, not a substitute for professional diagnosis.

A Note on Whole-Body Inflammation

While gastritis is a localized condition, chronic stress on the body, including from ongoing digestive problems, is part of your overall inflammatory picture.
