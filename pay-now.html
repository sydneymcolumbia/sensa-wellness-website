What Doctors Are Saying

The medical community has reached a consensus: chronic inflammation is a root cause of disease, and most people are never tested for it.

Inflammation is now recognized as a key mediator in the development of atherosclerosis. Measuring high-sensitivity CRP provides independent prognostic information beyond traditional risk factors and should be considered in the evaluation of patients at intermediate cardiovascular risk.

Dr. Paul M. Ridker, MD

Director, Center for Cardiovascular Disease Prevention, Brigham and Women's Hospital; Professor of Medicine, Harvard Medical School. Pioneer of the high-sensitivity CRP test.

Chronic, low-grade inflammation is a key driver of insulin resistance, obesity, and type 2 diabetes. Understanding and monitoring inflammatory markers gives patients and clinicians a powerful tool for early intervention, long before disease is diagnosed.

Dr. Mark Hyman, MD

Director, Cleveland Clinic Center for Functional Medicine; 14x New York Times Bestselling Author; Chairman, Institute for Functional Medicine

Most people think of inflammation as the redness and swelling after an injury. But chronic systemic inflammation (the kind you can't see or feel) is silently driving some of the most devastating diseases of our time, including Alzheimer's, cancer, and heart disease. Testing for it regularly should be standard practice.

Dr. Andrew Weil, MD

Founder and Director, University of Arizona Center for Integrative Medicine; Clinical Professor of Medicine, University of Arizona

CRP is arguably the most important biomarker most people have never heard of. Elevated levels are associated with significantly increased risk of cardiovascular events, metabolic dysfunction, and all-cause mortality. The fact that it can now be measured outside a clinical lab is a meaningful step forward for preventive care.

Dr. Peter Attia, MD

Founder, Early Medical; Expert in longevity medicine and performance; Former surgical oncology fellow, National Cancer Institute/NIH

Chronic Inflammation Has No Symptoms Until It's Too Late

Unlike acute inflammation (the kind that makes a cut turn red), chronic systemic inflammation is invisible. It doesn't hurt. It doesn't show up on your skin. It quietly damages blood vessels, disrupts hormones, impairs insulin signaling, and accelerates aging. By the time it causes a diagnosis, the damage has often been accumulating for years.

🢄

Heart Disease

Inflammation drives plaque buildup in arteries. CRP levels above 3 mg/L triple the risk of a heart attack.

🧠

Brain Health

Neuroinflammation is linked to depression, cognitive decline, and a significantly elevated risk of Alzheimer's disease.

⚖️

Weight & Metabolism

If you're eating well and exercising but still can't lose weight, inflammation may be the missing piece. Inflammatory cytokines impair insulin signaling, disrupt leptin and hunger hormones, and create a metabolic environment that actively resists fat loss, regardless of calorie intake.

🦴

Joints & Mobility

Chronic inflammation is the defining feature of rheumatoid arthritis and contributes to osteoarthritis progression and chronic pain.

🔋

Energy & Fatigue

High inflammation dysregulates the HPA axis and mitochondrial function, contributing to persistent fatigue and reduced physical performance.

🧬

Cancer Risk

Inflammatory microenvironments promote tumor growth. Chronic inflammation is a recognized risk factor for multiple cancer types.
