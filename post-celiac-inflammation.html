Celiac Disease and Inflammation

Celiac disease is a case where a single dietary trigger sets off an autoimmune attack on the gut. Here is how gluten drives intestinal inflammation, why diagnosis must come first, and what the only real treatment is.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

Celiac disease is a chronic autoimmune condition in which eating gluten, a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye, triggers an immune reaction that inflames and damages the lining of the small intestine. It affects roughly 1 percent of people worldwide, though most cases remain undiagnosed. The inflammation flattens the tiny finger-like villi that absorb nutrients, which can lead to deficiencies and a wide range of symptoms. The only effective treatment is a strict, lifelong gluten-free diet. Crucially, celiac disease must be diagnosed while you are still eating gluten, so you should not remove gluten from your diet before testing, because doing so can hide the disease and make an accurate diagnosis much harder.

Celiac disease sits at the intersection of diet, immunity, and inflammation. Unlike most autoimmune conditions, it has a clearly identified environmental trigger, gluten, which means the inflammation can be switched off by removing that trigger. That makes celiac unusual and, in one sense, hopeful. But it also comes with an important sequencing rule that people often get wrong: testing has to happen before you change your diet.

Celiac disease is an autoimmune disorder in which ingesting gluten prompts the immune system to attack the small intestine. It is distinct from a wheat allergy and from non-celiac gluten sensitivity, and it is defined by specific antibodies and by characteristic damage to the intestinal lining seen on biopsy.

How Gluten Drives Intestinal Inflammation

In people with celiac disease, who carry particular genetic markers, gluten sets off a specific and damaging immune response. When gluten is broken down in the gut, a fragment called gliadin interacts with an enzyme called tissue transglutaminase, and the immune system reacts as though these were dangerous invaders. This produces autoantibodies and recruits inflammatory immune cells into the lining of the small intestine. The result is ongoing inflammation localized to the gut, sustained for as long as gluten keeps arriving.

The consequence of that inflammation is structural. The small intestine is lined with villi, microscopic projections that hugely increase the surface area available for absorbing nutrients. Persistent celiac inflammation flattens and damages these villi, a change called villous atrophy. With fewer functioning villi, the gut absorbs nutrients poorly, which is why untreated celiac disease can cause deficiencies in iron, calcium, folate, and vitamins, along with digestive symptoms. This is inflammation with a clear cause and a clear physical cost.

Symptoms Are Wide-Ranging

Celiac disease is sometimes called a great imitator because its effects reach well beyond the gut. Classic digestive symptoms include diarrhea, bloating, abdominal pain, and weight loss. But many people, especially adults, have few or no digestive complaints and instead present with fatigue, iron-deficiency anemia, bone thinning, mouth ulcers, a blistering skin rash called dermatitis herpetiformis, or, in children, poor growth. This variability, together with the many silent cases, is a major reason celiac disease is so widely underdiagnosed. The table below outlines common presentations.

Why You Must Be Diagnosed Before Going Gluten-Free

This is the single most important practical point about celiac disease. Testing relies on detecting the immune response to gluten, so it only works accurately if gluten is still in your diet. The main screening tests measure antibodies such as tissue transglutaminase IgA, and the confirmatory step is usually an endoscopy with a small-bowel biopsy looking for villous damage. Both the antibodies and the intestinal damage begin to fade once gluten is removed. If you cut out gluten first and then get tested, the results can come back falsely normal even when you truly have celiac disease.

That creates a frustrating situation for anyone who has already gone gluten-free and feels better, because reintroducing gluten is often needed for valid testing, a step called a gluten challenge that should be done under medical guidance. The takeaway is straightforward: if you suspect celiac disease, see a healthcare provider and get tested before changing your diet. Self-diagnosing and self-treating with a gluten-free diet can rob you of an accurate diagnosis, which matters because celiac requires lifelong management and monitoring.

The Only Treatment: A Strict Gluten-Free Diet

Once celiac disease is confirmed, the treatment is clear and, at present, singular: a strict, lifelong gluten-free diet. Removing gluten allows the intestinal inflammation to settle and the villi to heal over months, which resolves symptoms and corrects nutrient absorption in most people. There is currently no approved medication that lets people with celiac disease eat gluten safely, though research into such therapies is ongoing. Strict means strict; even small amounts of gluten, including cross-contamination, can reignite the immune response and the inflammation.

Because a gluten-free diet requires careful attention to hidden sources of gluten in processed foods, sauces, and shared kitchen surfaces, most people benefit from working with a dietitian experienced in celiac disease. Ongoing follow-up with a clinician is also standard, both to confirm healing and to monitor for nutritional gaps. This is a manageable condition, but it is a medical one that calls for professional guidance rather than a do-it-yourself approach.

Celiac Is Not the Same as Gluten Sensitivity

An important source of confusion is the difference between celiac disease and other reactions to gluten or wheat. Celiac disease is a defined autoimmune condition with measurable antibodies and characteristic intestinal damage. Non-celiac gluten sensitivity describes people who report symptoms after eating gluten but do not have the antibodies or intestinal damage of celiac disease, and its mechanisms are still being studied. Wheat allergy is a third, separate condition, an allergic immune response that can cause rapid reactions. These distinctions matter because the risks and the required strictness differ. In celiac disease, even trace gluten reignites an autoimmune attack that can silently damage the gut, which is why lifelong strictness and formal diagnosis are essential rather than optional.

This is also why self-diagnosis is risky. Many people who cut gluten on their own feel better and assume they have celiac disease, when they may have a different and less demanding condition, or a placebo effect, or another cause entirely such as irritable bowel syndrome. Only proper testing can sort this out, and it must be done while gluten is still being eaten. Getting an accurate label changes how carefully a person needs to eat for the rest of their life and whether they need ongoing medical follow-up and screening of relatives.

Why Untreated Celiac Disease Matters

Because so many cases are silent or mild, it can be tempting to think an undiagnosed or loosely managed celiac disease is not a big deal. The evidence argues otherwise. Ongoing intestinal inflammation and villous damage impair nutrient absorption, contributing to iron-deficiency anemia, bone thinning, and other deficiency-related problems over time. Untreated celiac disease is also associated with a higher risk of certain long-term complications. This is why formal diagnosis is not just a bureaucratic step; it establishes whether a person needs the lifelong, strict, monitored dietary treatment that prevents these consequences. The reassuring flip side is that once diagnosed and treated with a strict gluten-free diet, most people heal well and thrive.

Celiac, Systemic Inflammation, and CRP

The inflammation in celiac disease is centered in the gut, and standard blood markers of systemic inflammation are not how it is diagnosed or monitored. C-reactive protein (CRP) is a general marker that can be normal in people with celiac disease, so a normal CRP does not rule it out, and a raised CRP does not confirm it. Celiac diagnosis and monitoring rely on celiac-specific antibody tests and biopsy, not on CRP. That said, chronic gut inflammation and the nutritional consequences of untreated celiac disease can affect overall health, which is one reason accurate diagnosis and treatment matter beyond the digestive symptoms alone.
