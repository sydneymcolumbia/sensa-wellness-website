How Do You Reduce Inflammation Quickly?

The honest answer depends on which kind of inflammation you mean. Acute inflammation can settle in days. Chronic, low-grade inflammation is a different problem with a slower timeline, and mixing up the two is where most quick-fix advice goes wrong.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

You can reduce acute inflammation relatively quickly with rest, quality sleep, hydration, gentle movement, and anti-inflammatory foods, and CRP from a passing trigger falls on its own within a day or two thanks to its short half-life of about 19 hours. Chronic, low-grade inflammation cannot be fixed overnight. It responds over weeks to months to sustained changes in diet, weight, activity, sleep, and stress. Be skeptical of anything promising to erase chronic inflammation in 24 hours.

The phrase reduce inflammation quickly hides an important distinction. Inflammation is not one thing. Acute inflammation is the body's short-term response to an injury, infection, or intense exercise, and it is designed to resolve once the trigger is gone. Chronic inflammation is a persistent, low-grade activation that builds over months and years. What reduces one quickly does not necessarily touch the other, and understanding which you are dealing with is the difference between realistic action and chasing a fantasy.

Acute inflammation is a short-term immune response that resolves in days once its trigger clears. Chronic inflammation is a persistent low-grade state that develops over months to years and responds only to sustained lifestyle change.

What Reduces Inflammation Quickly (and Genuinely Works)

For acute inflammation or a recent flare, several things do help within a short window. These work by supporting the body's natural resolution process rather than by magically switching inflammation off.

LeverHow it helps quickly
SleepEven one good night reduces the inflammatory signaling that short sleep drives up
HydrationSupports circulation and clearance; dehydration adds physiological stress
Gentle movementLight activity aids lymphatic flow and recovery without adding new stress
Anti-inflammatory foodsOmega-3s and polyphenols support the resolution phase of inflammation

Prioritize a good night of sleep. Sleep is one of the fastest levers because short and fragmented sleep measurably raises inflammatory markers, sometimes within days, and correcting it reverses that. See sleep and inflammation.

Eat anti-inflammatory foods. The best-studied anti-inflammatory compounds are omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA), curcumin from turmeric, and dietary polyphenols. Omega-3s generate specialized pro-resolving mediators that help actively shut down inflammatory responses, and curcumin blocks the master inflammatory switch NF-kB. A meal built around fatty fish, colorful vegetables, olive oil, nuts, and berries supports resolution. See omega-3s, curcumin, and polyphenols.

Hydrate and move gently. Adequate hydration and light activity such as walking support circulation and recovery. Intense exercise, by contrast, is a temporary pro-inflammatory stimulus, so during an acute flare the goal is gentle movement, not a hard workout.

Remove ongoing triggers. Cutting alcohol and ultra-processed food for a stretch removes pro-inflammatory input and lets the body resolve faster. See alcohol and processed foods.

Why Chronic Inflammation Cannot Be Reduced Overnight

Here is what the quick-fix content leaves out. If your inflammation is chronic, driven by visceral fat, ongoing stress, poor metabolic health, or a persistently inflammatory diet, there is no 24-hour solution, because the drivers themselves take time to change. Losing meaningful visceral fat, remodeling your dietary pattern, building an exercise habit, and repairing chronic sleep debt are all measured in weeks and months, not hours. The encouraging news is that the payoff is real and quantifiable: pooled trials show the Mediterranean diet lowers hs-CRP by nearly 1 mg/L, and CRP drops by roughly 0.13 mg/dL for every kilogram of weight lost. Those are meaningful shifts, they are just not instant. For the full playbook, see how to lower CRP.

What Does Not Work as Advertised

A quick reality check on common claims. Single foods and supplements marketed as inflammation erasers rarely produce dramatic changes on their own; dietary pattern over time matters far more than any one ingredient. Cleanses and detox teas do not reduce inflammation in any validated way. And feeling better after a change is not the same as your inflammation actually falling, because symptoms are unreliable and can improve or worsen independent of your underlying inflammatory state. This gap between what you feel and what is measurably happening is exactly why an objective marker is so useful.

Setting the Right Expectations

The single most useful mental shift when trying to reduce inflammation is to match your expectations to the type you are dealing with. If you have an acute flare, a recent illness, a hard workout, a minor injury, expect it to settle over days as your body resolves it, and use rest, sleep, hydration, and anti-inflammatory foods to support that process. If you are addressing a chronic baseline, expect a timeline of weeks to months and judge success by the trend across multiple measurements rather than by how you feel on any given day. The reason this matters is that mismatched expectations lead people to abandon effective changes too early, concluding that a diet or exercise habit failed when in truth it simply had not been given the weeks it needs. Realistic expectations, paired with measurement, are what keep sustainable changes in place long enough to work. Quick relief and lasting reduction are different goals with different timelines, and confusing them is the most common reason people give up.

How Inflammation Actually Resolves

Understanding why acute inflammation can settle quickly, while chronic inflammation cannot, requires knowing that resolution is an active process, not just inflammation fading away. When the immune system mounts an acute response, it simultaneously sets in motion the machinery to shut it down, releasing specialized pro-resolving mediators, some of which are derived from omega-3 fatty acids, that actively terminate the inflammatory signal and clear debris. This is why supporting resolution with sleep, hydration, and omega-3-rich foods can speed the settling of an acute flare. Chronic inflammation, by contrast, represents a failure of this resolution process. The off-switch is not working properly because the drivers, excess visceral fat, ongoing stress, poor sleep, an inflammatory diet, keep sending the on-signal. That is the fundamental reason chronic inflammation resists quick fixes: you are not waiting for a response to resolve, you are removing the inputs that prevent resolution in the first place.

What About Cold, Heat, and Movement?

Beyond food and sleep, several physical strategies are popular for reducing inflammation, and the evidence is mixed but informative. Gentle movement genuinely aids recovery from an acute flare by supporting circulation and lymphatic flow, whereas intense exercise is itself a temporary pro-inflammatory stimulus and is best avoided during a flare. Cold exposure and heat exposure such as sauna are studied for their effects on inflammatory markers, and both have plausible mechanisms, though effects vary and they are not instant cures. See cold exposure and sauna for the details. The honest framing for all of these is the same as for food: they can support the body's own resolution of acute inflammation and contribute to a lower chronic baseline over time, but none flips inflammation off like a switch.

A Sensible Short-Term Reset

If you want a concrete way to support your body during an acute flare or a stretch of feeling inflamed, a short reset built on the fundamentals is reasonable, with the clear understanding that it supports resolution rather than forcing it. For a few days, prioritize seven to nine hours of sleep, hydrate well, build meals around fatty fish, vegetables, olive oil, and berries, cut alcohol and ultra-processed foods, and keep movement gentle. This is not a detox or a cure, and it will not erase a chronic baseline. What it does is remove ongoing pro-inflammatory inputs and supply the raw materials your body uses to resolve inflammation, which is genuinely the best you can do in the short term. For lasting change, the same principles simply need to become sustained habits rather than a few-day effort, as covered in how to lower CRP.
