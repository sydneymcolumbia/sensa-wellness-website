How Do You Reduce Inflammation in Your Body?

Chronic low-grade inflammation responds to how you eat, move, sleep, and manage stress. The good news is that the biggest levers are also the most ordinary. Here is what the evidence supports and how to sequence it.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

You reduce chronic inflammation in the body through six evidence-based levers: eat an anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean-style, high in fiber, vegetables, and healthy fats), exercise regularly, lose excess body fat, prioritize sleep, stop smoking, and manage chronic stress. A meta-analysis of randomized trials found the Mediterranean diet produced the most consistent reductions in inflammatory markers including C-reactive protein and interleukin-6. These levers add together, so combining several lowers inflammation more than perfecting any single one. Most people see measurable changes in markers like CRP within weeks to a few months.

Reducing inflammation in the body is less about any single miracle food or supplement and more about steadily removing the everyday triggers that keep the immune system switched on. Chronic low-grade inflammation is maintained by an inflammatory diet, excess fat, inactivity, smoking, poor sleep, and unmanaged stress, so the way to lower it is to work on those same factors. Because they overlap and reinforce each other, progress on one often makes the others easier.

Reducing inflammation means lowering chronic, low-grade immune activation through lifestyle change: an anti-inflammatory diet, regular movement, healthy body weight, quality sleep, no smoking, and stress management. These are the levers with the strongest evidence for moving markers like C-reactive protein.

What Is the Most Effective Way to Reduce Inflammation?

The single most effective evidence-based change is adopting a Mediterranean-style diet. According to a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in Advances in Nutrition, the Mediterranean diet produced the most prominent reductions in inflammatory biomarkers of any dietary pattern studied, lowering interleukin-6 by about 1.07 pg/mL, interleukin-1-beta, and C-reactive protein by roughly 1.00 mg/L. This way of eating emphasizes vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, olive oil, and fish, while minimizing red and processed meat, added sugar, and ultra-processed foods. It works because it is high in fiber and polyphenols and low in the components that provoke inflammation. See our anti-inflammatory diet guide for how to put it into practice.

How Does Exercise Reduce Inflammation?

Regular exercise lowers inflammation both by reducing body fat and through direct anti-inflammatory effects of muscle activity. Working muscles release signaling molecules that shift the immune system toward a less inflammatory state, and consistent activity is associated with lower resting levels of C-reactive protein over time. A single hard workout briefly raises inflammation as part of normal recovery, but the long-term adaptation to regular training is a lower baseline. Both aerobic exercise and resistance training contribute, and the biggest gains come from moving from sedentary to active rather than from pushing to extremes. For most people, a mix of brisk activity most days plus a couple of strength sessions a week is a sound target. See our post on exercise and inflammation.

Does Losing Weight Reduce Inflammation?

Yes, reducing excess body fat is one of the most powerful ways to lower inflammation. Fat tissue, especially visceral fat around the organs, is metabolically active and secretes inflammatory cytokines directly, so carrying excess fat keeps the immune system chronically stimulated. Losing weight reduces this source of inflammatory signaling, and studies consistently show that weight loss lowers C-reactive protein. Because diet and exercise both support fat loss and independently reduce inflammation, they compound one another. The goal does not have to be dramatic: even modest, sustained fat loss meaningfully improves inflammatory markers. See how obesity and inflammation are linked.

How Do Sleep and Stress Affect Inflammation?

Poor sleep and chronic stress both raise inflammation, so improving them lowers it. Sleep deprivation dysregulates immune signaling and is associated with higher inflammatory markers, which is why a consistent 7 to 9 hours matters. Chronic psychological stress keeps stress hormones and inflammatory pathways elevated, and practices that calm the stress response appear to help. According to a meta-analysis of 42 randomized trials in Psychoneuroendocrinology, interventions that included yoga were associated with improved regulation of the stress-response systems, including reductions in cortisol. Mindfulness, breathing practices, and regular downtime work through similar routes. These levers are easy to underrate but genuinely move the needle. See sleep and inflammation and how stress drives inflammation.

Why Quitting Smoking Matters

Stopping smoking is one of the clearest anti-inflammatory changes a person can make. Tobacco smoke delivers a continuous stream of oxidative and chemical damage that keeps the immune system activated, and smokers consistently show higher inflammatory markers than non-smokers. The encouraging news is that quitting reverses much of this over time, with inflammatory markers declining after cessation. If you smoke, quitting is likely to do more for your inflammatory baseline than any dietary tweak. See smoking and inflammation for the details.

Do Supplements Help Reduce Inflammation?

Some supplements have evidence for modestly reducing inflammation, but they are secondary to the core lifestyle levers and vary widely in quality of evidence. Omega-3 fatty acids from fish oil have among the better track records for lowering inflammatory markers, and correcting a genuine vitamin D deficiency may help those who are deficient. Concentrated curcumin and other plant polyphenols have shown reductions in C-reactive protein in trials, though usually at doses higher than food provides. The honest framing is that no supplement rivals the impact of an anti-inflammatory diet, regular exercise, healthy weight, good sleep, and not smoking; supplements can complement those foundations but not replace them. Because some supplements interact with medications, discuss any concentrated supplement with a healthcare provider first. See our posts on omega-3s and inflammation and vitamin D and inflammation.

What Foods Should You Avoid to Reduce Inflammation?

To reduce inflammation, the foods most worth limiting are added sugar, refined carbohydrates, ultra-processed foods, processed meats, and excess alcohol. These are the components that most consistently push inflammatory markers in the wrong direction, largely through repeated blood-sugar spikes, poor fat quality, and effects on the gut and metabolism. You do not need a perfect diet or a list of forbidden foods; the goal is to shift the everyday default toward whole, minimally processed items and away from the ultra-processed ones. Cutting sugary drinks alone is one of the highest-impact single changes many people can make. For the full picture of what to minimize, see our guide to inflammatory foods, and for what to eat more of, see the Mediterranean diet and inflammation.

How Long Until Inflammation Comes Down?

Most people see measurable reductions in inflammatory markers within weeks to a few months of sustained lifestyle change. Because chronic inflammation is maintained by ongoing triggers, removing them allows markers like C-reactive protein to fall, and CRP responds quickly enough that changes are often visible within weeks. The exact timeline varies with which levers you change and how consistently, but the direction is reliable: reduce the triggers, and the baseline follows. This is also why measuring is so useful, because it turns a slow, invisible process into visible feedback. For faster short-term tactics, see how to reduce inflammation fast.

Can You Reduce Inflammation Quickly, or Does It Take Time?

You can influence inflammation in the short term, but durable reduction of a chronically elevated baseline takes sustained change over weeks to months. Short-term steps like a few days of better sleep, hydration, gentle movement, and an anti-inflammatory way of eating can nudge markers, and resolving an acute trigger brings a fast drop. But the low-grade inflammation associated with long-term health is maintained by ongoing habits, so lowering it durably means changing those habits consistently rather than chasing a quick fix. The most productive mindset is to treat inflammation reduction as a trend you steer over time, not a switch you flip. Rapid crash approaches rarely hold, whereas modest changes you can maintain compound into a genuinely lower baseline. Tracking Whether You Are Actually Reducing Inflammation

The only way to know whether your efforts are working is to measure a marker over time, because inflammation cannot be felt directly. C-reactive protein is the most practical choice, since it responds to diet, exercise, sleep, and weight change within days to weeks and uses a single standard scale.
