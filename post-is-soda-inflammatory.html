Is Soda Inflammatory?

Sugar-sweetened soda is one of the clearer villains in the inflammation story. Unlike most food claims, this one is backed by consistent human evidence. Here is what the research shows and why the mechanism makes sense.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

Yes, sugar-sweetened soda is genuinely pro-inflammatory, and this is one of the better-supported claims in nutrition. A randomized controlled trial found that even low to moderate intake of sugar-sweetened beverages worsened markers of cardiovascular risk, including high-sensitivity CRP, in healthy young men within about three weeks. Observational studies consistently link higher soda intake with elevated inflammatory markers, especially in people with obesity or prediabetes. The mechanism is clear: large, rapid loads of sugar drive blood-sugar spikes, fat accumulation, and inflammatory signaling.

Most foods marketed as anti-inflammatory rest on thin evidence, but sugar-sweetened soda sits at the opposite end: it is one of the few dietary items with a strong, consistent, and mechanistically sensible case for being pro-inflammatory. When people ask whether soda is inflammatory, the honest answer is a clear yes for the sugar-sweetened kind, and the useful detail is how quickly and by what pathways it acts. This is not scaremongering; it is one of the better-established links between a specific food and inflammation.

Sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) are drinks with added sugar, including regular soda, sweetened iced teas, energy drinks, and many fruit drinks. Their defining feature is delivering a large dose of rapidly absorbed sugar with little else.

Is Soda Inflammatory?

Sugar-sweetened soda is pro-inflammatory, and it works through several reinforcing pathways rather than one. A can of regular soda delivers a large bolus of sugar, often around 35 to 40 grams, with no fiber, protein, or fat to slow absorption. That produces a sharp blood-sugar and insulin spike, and repeated spikes promote inflammatory signaling. The fructose portion of the sugar is processed in the liver and can drive fat accumulation and metabolic stress. Over time, regular soda intake contributes to weight gain and visceral fat, which is itself an active source of inflammatory cytokines. Each of these pathways points the same way, which is why the pro-inflammatory case for soda is unusually solid.

What Does the Research Show?

The strongest evidence comes from both randomized trials and large cohort studies pointing in the same direction. A randomized controlled trial in healthy young men found that consuming low to moderate amounts of sugar-sweetened beverages impaired glucose and lipid metabolism and raised markers of cardiovascular risk, including high-sensitivity CRP, within roughly three weeks. This is notable because it shows harm from amounts many people would consider modest, and because a randomized design supports causation rather than mere association. On the observational side, NHANES analyses of US adults link higher SSB intake with elevated CRP, with especially strong associations in people who have abdominal obesity or prediabetes.

The honest reading is that soda's pro-inflammatory effect is well supported by multiple lines of evidence, including at least one randomized trial, which is a higher bar than most dietary claims clear. Diet soda, which lacks the sugar, does not carry the same clear inflammatory signal, though its long-term health effects remain an area of ongoing study. The concern here is specifically the added sugar.

It is worth appreciating how unusual this level of evidence is in nutrition, because it should shape how confidently we act on it. Most claims about individual foods rest on observational data alone, where people who eat a certain food differ from those who do not in dozens of ways that are hard to fully separate. Sugar-sweetened beverages are one of the few dietary exposures that have been tested in controlled human experiments, precisely because it is feasible and ethical to ask volunteers to drink a defined amount of a sugary beverage for a few weeks and measure what happens. That those experiments found unfavorable changes in inflammatory and metabolic markers, in the same direction as the large observational studies, is what elevates the soda story from plausible to well supported. When mechanism, randomized trials, and population data all agree, the conclusion deserves more confidence than usual.

Why Sugar Drives Inflammation

The reason liquid sugar is especially problematic comes down to how fast and how completely it is absorbed. Because soda has no fiber, protein, or fat to slow digestion, its sugar hits the bloodstream quickly, producing steep glucose and insulin surges. Fructose, which makes up about half of the sugar in most sodas, is handled largely by the liver, where in excess it promotes fat production and metabolic stress linked to inflammation. Chronically high sugar intake also contributes to weight gain and visceral fat, and visceral fat behaves like an inflammatory organ, releasing cytokines such as IL-6 and TNF-alpha. The combination of rapid glucose spikes, hepatic fat, and expanding visceral fat is why sugar in liquid form is a particularly effective driver of low-grade inflammation.

Is It the Sugar or the Soda?

The inflammatory problem is the added sugar delivered in liquid form, not the carbonation or the soda format itself. Sparkling water without sugar carries no such concern, and the same inflammatory issues apply to other sugar-sweetened drinks like sweetened teas, energy drinks, and many fruit-flavored beverages. Liquid sugar appears to be worse than the equivalent sugar in solid food because drinks do not trigger the same fullness, so people tend to add these calories on top of what they already eat rather than compensating for them. This is why sugary drinks are singled out in dietary guidance even among sources of added sugar.

What About Diet Soda?

Diet sodas, which use non-nutritive sweeteners instead of sugar, do not carry the same clear inflammatory signal, because the central driver, a large sugar load, is absent. That said, the long-term health effects of artificial sweeteners are still being studied, and diet soda is best viewed as a lower-harm swap rather than a health food. For someone trying to reduce inflammation, replacing regular soda with water, sparkling water, or unsweetened drinks is the strongest move, with diet soda as a reasonable transitional step for people cutting back on sugar. The clear, evidence-based target is reducing sugar-sweetened beverages.

How Much Soda Is Too Much?

People often want a safe threshold, and the honest answer is that there is no established safe level of sugar-sweetened soda, only degrees of risk that rise with intake. The randomized evidence is sobering precisely because it found metabolic and inflammatory changes at low to moderate intakes, not just at extreme ones, which undercuts the idea that a daily can is harmless. Dietary guidance frames added sugar in terms of an upper limit rather than a target, with the American Heart Association suggesting most women keep added sugar under about 25 grams a day and most men under about 36 grams. A single 12-ounce can of regular soda can meet or exceed that entire daily allowance on its own, leaving no room for the added sugar in the rest of a typical diet. This is why sugary drinks are singled out: they can quietly consume a person's whole sugar budget in a few minutes of drinking.

How Cutting Soda Fits an Anti-Inflammatory Diet

Among all the dietary changes a person can make for inflammation, cutting sugar-sweetened soda is one of the highest-value and best-supported. It removes a concentrated, fiber-free sugar load that drives blood-sugar spikes and visceral fat, and it does so without requiring any new food to be added. Because the randomized evidence shows effects within weeks, this is also one of the changes where results can appear relatively quickly. Replacing daily soda with water or unsweetened drinks is a simple, sustainable, and evidence-backed step that complements the rest of an anti-inflammatory pattern.

Tracking Whether Cutting Soda Lowers Your Inflammation

Because the effect of sugar-sweetened soda on inflammation is relatively fast and well documented, this is one of the better dietary changes to track with measurement. C-reactive protein (CRP) is the most widely used blood marker of inflammation, and it responds to dietary change within days to weeks, which fits the timeframe in which soda's effects appear. A practical approach is to take a couple of baseline readings while you are still drinking soda, cut it out while holding your other habits steady, and watch the trend across the following weeks. Because CRP responds to lifestyle within days to weeks and clears quickly, a change of this kind is among the more likely to show up in a series of readings, and a run of measurements tells a far more honest story than any single test.
