What Is a Normal CRP Level?

CRP is the most widely used blood marker of inflammation, but the number only means something once you know which test was run and what range applies to you. Here is the full reference chart, plus how levels shift with age.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

A normal high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) level is below 1.0 mg/L, which the American Heart Association and CDC classify as low cardiovascular risk. A reading of 1.0 to 3.0 mg/L is average risk, and above 3.0 mg/L is high risk. For standard CRP, results below about 10 mg/L are usually considered normal. Levels above 10 mg/L typically signal an acute infection or injury rather than chronic low-grade inflammation.

C-reactive protein (CRP) is one of the most frequently ordered blood tests in the world, and yet a CRP result handed back without context tells you very little. The same number can be reassuring or concerning depending on which assay was used, how old you are, whether you recently had an infection, and where your reading sits relative to your own past results. Understanding what counts as normal starts with understanding that there are two different CRP tests measuring two different things.

Normal CRP depends on the assay. For high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP), used to assess chronic low-grade inflammation, below 1.0 mg/L is optimal. For standard CRP, used to detect acute inflammation, below roughly 10 mg/L is the usual normal cutoff.

What Is a Normal CRP Level on the Standard Chart?

A normal high-sensitivity CRP level is below 1.0 mg/L. The American Heart Association and the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention established a three-tier risk framework in their 2003 joint statement that remains the reference standard today. These categories were designed specifically for cardiovascular risk stratification, but they have become the everyday language for interpreting inflammation more broadly.

The 10 mg/L threshold matters because it marks the boundary between the two clinical uses of CRP. Below 10 mg/L, small differences in CRP reflect the chronic, smoldering inflammation associated with cardiovascular and metabolic health. Above 10 mg/L, CRP is usually responding to something acute, a chest infection, a urinary tract infection, a recent surgery, a flare of an autoimmune condition. Bacterial infections in particular can push CRP into the range of 100 mg/L or higher. When a reading lands above 10 mg/L, guidelines recommend treating it as an acute signal and repeating the test once you have recovered rather than interpreting it as your baseline.

Standard CRP vs High-Sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP)

The difference between the two CRP tests is sensitivity, not the protein being measured. Standard CRP assays reliably detect levels above roughly 5 to 10 mg/L and are ordered when a clinician suspects active infection, a disease flare, or significant tissue injury. High-sensitivity CRP assays detect concentrations as low as about 0.1 to 0.5 mg/L, which is exactly the range where chronic low-grade inflammation lives. For anyone tracking inflammation for general wellness, longevity, or cardiovascular awareness, hs-CRP is the relevant test because the meaningful differences happen below the threshold a standard assay can even see.

Do Normal CRP Levels Change With Age?

CRP levels tend to rise gradually with age, even in people without acute illness. Large population data from the U.S. National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey, analyzed in the New England Journal of Medicine, found median CRP climbing from about 1.4 mg/L in adults aged 20 to 29 to roughly 2.7 mg/L in those 80 and older. This upward drift is part of what researchers call inflammaging, the slow accumulation of low-grade inflammation that accompanies biological aging.

Important nuance: the standard AHA and CDC risk categories are not formally age-adjusted. A reading of 2.5 mg/L is classified as average risk whether you are 30 or 70. But the age trend explains why a 25-year-old and a 75-year-old with identical lifestyles may show different baseline readings, and it is one more reason your personal trend over time is more informative than a one-off comparison to a population average. Women also tend to run slightly higher CRP than men on average, partly due to hormonal factors and body composition differences.

What Counts as an Optimal CRP Level?

While below 1.0 mg/L is the formal low-risk cutoff, many longevity and preventive-health practitioners aim lower still, often citing a target under 0.5 mg/L or even below 1.0 mg/L consistently sustained. The rationale is that CRP behaves as a continuous variable: within the low-risk band, lower generally tracks with a healthier inflammatory profile. There is no official guideline defining an optimal number below 1.0, so treat sub-1.0 targets as a reasonable aspiration rather than a validated clinical threshold. What is well established is the direction: moving your baseline down and keeping it stable is the goal.

Why a Single CRP Reading Can Mislead

CRP is produced by the liver in response to interleukin-6 (IL-6) and rises within 6 to 8 hours of an inflammatory trigger, peaking around 48 hours. Because it also clears quickly, with a circulating half-life of roughly 19 hours, CRP is highly responsive to recent events. A cold, a poor night of sleep, a hard workout, a minor injury, or a dental procedure can all transiently push your number up. That responsiveness is what makes CRP useful for tracking, but it also means any single reading is a snapshot, not a verdict.

This is precisely why the CDC recommends taking two hs-CRP measurements about two weeks apart and averaging them for cardiovascular risk assessment in metabolically stable people. If either reading exceeds 10 mg/L, the guidance is to pause, assume an acute process, and retest after recovery. For everyday wellness tracking, the same logic applies: your baseline is best understood as a trend across several readings, not a verdict from one.

Because CRP responds to lifestyle within days to weeks, it is one of the few blood markers where frequent, low-friction measurement genuinely adds value. What Can Make a Normal CRP Reading Look Abnormal?

Because CRP is so responsive, several ordinary circumstances can push a genuinely healthy person's reading temporarily out of their normal range, and recognizing them prevents needless worry. A cold or other minor infection you barely notice can elevate CRP for several days. A hard or unaccustomed workout in the day or two before testing can raise it. Recent dental work, a minor injury, or a poor stretch of sleep can nudge it up. Certain medications and, in some people, hormonal factors can also influence the number. None of these mean your true baseline has changed. They are exactly why guidelines emphasize averaging readings and testing away from acute events. If a single result looks higher than expected, the most useful response is to note whether any of these applied and retest under calmer conditions.

How CRP Compares With Other Inflammation Markers

CRP is the most widely used inflammation marker, but it is not the only one, and understanding its place helps you interpret a normal result. ESR (erythrocyte sedimentation rate) is the traditional alternative, but it responds far more slowly and its normal range shifts with age and sex, which makes it less suited to precise tracking. IL-6, the cytokine that actually triggers CRP production, is measured mainly in research. Fibrinogen and ferritin can reflect inflammation in specific clinical contexts. For everyday interpretation of what is normal, CRP wins on three counts: it is reported on a single standard scale that means the same thing for everyone, it responds quickly enough to reflect recent change, and it can be measured from a small blood sample.
