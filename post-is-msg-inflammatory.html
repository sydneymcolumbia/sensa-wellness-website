Is MSG Inflammatory?

MSG is one of the most unfairly maligned ingredients in the kitchen. Decades of controlled human research have largely cleared it, and much of its bad reputation traces to a myth rather than to evidence. Here is what the science actually shows.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

No, MSG is largely exonerated in controlled human studies, and it is not a meaningful inflammatory food at normal dietary amounts. Monosodium glutamate is recognized as safe (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, and controlled trials have failed to confirm the reactions long blamed on it, including the so-called Chinese Restaurant Syndrome. In 2018 the International Headache Society even removed MSG from its list of headache triggers for lack of evidence. The real caveats are that MSG contains sodium and that it often appears in ultra-processed foods, so the food around it matters more than the MSG itself.

MSG has one of the widest gaps between reputation and evidence of any food ingredient. Monosodium glutamate is the sodium salt of glutamate, an amino acid found naturally in tomatoes, parmesan, mushrooms, and many other foods. Its bad name traces largely to an anecdotal 1968 letter that coined Chinese Restaurant Syndrome, which set off decades of fear that controlled science has never been able to substantiate. Being honest about the evidence means saying clearly that MSG is not the inflammatory villain popular culture made it.

Monosodium glutamate (MSG) is the sodium salt of glutamate, a common amino acid that occurs naturally in many foods and gives the savory umami taste. The body handles added glutamate the same way it handles the glutamate naturally present in food.

Does MSG Cause Inflammation?

At normal dietary amounts, MSG does not appear to cause meaningful inflammation in humans. Controlled human studies have not confirmed the symptoms historically attributed to MSG, and reviews of the evidence conclude that MSG is safe at the levels people actually consume. Much of the alarming data comes from animal studies that injected very high doses of MSG directly, bypassing normal digestion, which does not reflect how humans eat it in food. When glutamate is eaten as part of a meal, the gut metabolizes most of it, and it behaves like the glutamate naturally present in tomatoes and cheese.

There is some laboratory interest in whether very high MSG intake could affect the gut microbiome or metabolism, and this remains an active research area worth watching. But short-term human and cell studies at realistic doses have generally not shown MSG raising inflammatory markers, and one animal study found that a modest MSG dose did not increase C-reactive protein compared with controls. The weight of the human evidence points toward exoneration, not condemnation.

Where Did the MSG Fear Come From?

The MSG scare is a case study in how anecdote can outrun evidence. In 1968 a letter to a medical journal described symptoms after Chinese meals and speculated MSG was the cause, coining a syndrome that stuck culturally for decades. Rigorous double-blind studies that followed repeatedly failed to reproduce consistent reactions when people did not know whether they were getting MSG or a placebo. In 2018 the International Headache Society removed MSG from its list of causative factors for headache, citing lack of evidence. The persistence of the fear, despite the science, is largely a story about reputation rather than physiology.

The Real Caveats Worth Knowing

Being honest also means naming the small, real caveats rather than pretending MSG is a health food. First, MSG contains sodium, though notably less per gram than table salt, so it still contributes to overall sodium intake, which matters for blood pressure and possibly for inflammation through emerging pathways. Second, MSG is frequently added to ultra-processed and fast foods, which are themselves linked to inflammation. In those cases the inflammatory signal comes from the refined carbs, fried components, and additives in the overall food, not from the MSG. Third, a small number of people report genuine sensitivity to large doses, which is worth respecting individually even though it is not a general population effect.

Is MSG Different from the Glutamate in Whole Foods?

Chemically, the glutamate in added MSG is the same molecule as the glutamate that occurs naturally in food. A ripe tomato, aged parmesan, soy sauce, mushrooms, and human breast milk all contain free glutamate. The body does not distinguish between glutamate from a shaker of MSG and glutamate from these whole foods, and it metabolizes both the same way. This is one of the clearest reasons the fear does not hold up: singling out added MSG as uniquely harmful ignores that we eat far more glutamate from ordinary foods every day.

Why the MSG Myth Has Been So Persistent

It is worth understanding why a debunked idea has lasted so long, because the answer offers a useful lesson for reading nutrition claims in general. The MSG scare took hold at a time when the ingredient was strongly associated with a specific cuisine, and researchers and commentators have noted that this association carried cultural bias that made the fear stickier than the evidence warranted. Once a symptom label like Chinese Restaurant Syndrome enters popular culture, people begin attributing ordinary post-meal sensations to it, creating a self-reinforcing expectation that is hard to dislodge even when controlled studies come back negative.

This pattern, where a vivid anecdote outpaces careful science and then resists correction, is common in food folklore. It is a reminder that the strength of a belief about a food does not reliably track the strength of the evidence behind it. For someone genuinely focused on inflammation, the most productive approach is to concentrate on the dietary patterns that are well supported, such as limiting ultra-processed and fried foods and emphasizing vegetables, fiber, and whole foods, rather than policing individual flagged ingredients like MSG that the evidence has largely cleared.

There is also a practical upside to letting go of MSG fear. Because glutamate delivers the savory umami taste, a small amount of MSG can make vegetables, soups, and lean proteins more appealing while using less added table salt, since MSG contains only about a third of the sodium of salt by weight. Some researchers have pointed out that, used this way, MSG could even help people reduce overall sodium intake without sacrificing flavor. That is a modest benefit rather than a health claim, but it underscores how far the reality is from the ingredient's fearful reputation.

Does MSG Affect People With Existing Conditions?

A reasonable follow-up is whether people with existing health conditions should treat MSG differently, and here the honest answer is that the evidence still does not single MSG out. Individuals sometimes report sensitivity, and a small subset may genuinely react to large doses taken on an empty stomach, which is worth respecting on a personal level. But for people managing inflammatory or autoimmune conditions, controlled research has not shown that normal dietary MSG meaningfully worsens their inflammation. The far more relevant factors are the overall dietary pattern, body weight, sleep, and activity, all of which influence inflammatory markers much more than a savory seasoning does.

If you suspect you personally react to MSG, the sensible approach is the same one that applies to any food question: test it rather than assume it. A structured trial, removing and then reintroducing the ingredient while paying attention to how you actually feel, gives you real information about your own body instead of relying on a decades-old reputation. For most people, that experiment ends with the reassuring conclusion that MSG is not the problem it was made out to be.

The Bottom Line on MSG and Inflammation

MSG is not a meaningful inflammatory food, and the evidence supports treating it as safe at normal dietary amounts. Controlled human studies have not confirmed the reactions blamed on it, the FDA recognizes it as safe, and the glutamate in MSG is identical to the glutamate in tomatoes and cheese. The real considerations are its sodium content and the fact that it often appears in ultra-processed foods that carry their own inflammatory load. If you are focused on inflammation, the foods MSG shows up in matter far more than the MSG itself.
