What is CRP?

C-reactive protein (CRP) is a protein your liver releases in response to inflammation. It is one of the most widely used markers of systemic inflammation, and tracking it over time can offer a clear picture of how your lifestyle is affecting your body from the inside.

The Basics

Understanding C-Reactive Protein

C-reactive protein is a protein produced by the liver. Under normal conditions, it circulates in the blood at very low concentrations. When the body detects tissue damage, infection, or sustained stress, immune signaling molecules called cytokines (particularly IL-6) prompt the liver to rapidly increase CRP production.

This makes CRP a reliable signal that something in the body has triggered an inflammatory response. In the short term, that response is helpful: it helps fight infections and heal injuries. The concern arises when inflammation becomes chronic, running quietly in the background for months or years without an obvious cause.

High-sensitivity CRP (hsCRP) assays are capable of detecting very small changes in CRP concentration, making them useful for tracking the kind of low-grade inflammation associated with everyday lifestyle factors like diet, sleep, stress, and physical activity.

How CRP Gets Produced

1

A stressor (infection, poor diet, poor sleep, chronic stress) activates immune cells.

2

Those cells release IL-6 and other pro-inflammatory cytokines into the bloodstream.

3

The liver detects these signals and ramps up CRP production within 4 to 6 hours.

4

CRP levels in the blood rise, providing a measurable signal of the body's inflammatory state.

Why It Matters

Why Track Your CRP?

A single CRP reading is informative. A trend over time is transformative. Regular monitoring gives you objective feedback on how your habits are affecting your body at a biological level.

01

Weight and Body Composition

Visceral fat (fat around internal organs) is one of the strongest drivers of elevated CRP. Tracking CRP alongside weight gives you a fuller picture of whether changes in body composition are actually reducing inflammation, not just moving the number on a scale.

02

Exercise and Recovery

Acute exercise transiently raises CRP, but consistent training lowers baseline inflammation over time. Monitoring CRP can help you find the balance between productive training stress and overtraining, and confirm your recovery strategies are working.

03

Energy and Sleep

Chronic fatigue and poor sleep quality are both associated with elevated CRP. Seeing your CRP fall as you prioritize sleep hygiene provides tangible confirmation that the effort is paying off beyond just feeling rested.

04

Long-Term Wellness

Chronic low-grade inflammation is a shared feature of many common conditions, including cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and accelerated aging. Keeping CRP in a healthy range is one measurable strategy for supporting long-term health.

05

Diet and Nutrition

Ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates, and excess sugar reliably raise CRP. Anti-inflammatory eating patterns consistently lower it. Your CRP can tell you whether a dietary change is working for your biology specifically, not just in population studies.

06

Stress and Mental Wellbeing

Psychological stress and social isolation both elevate inflammatory markers. Practices like consistent exercise, connection, and relaxation techniques that lower your perceived stress can also show up as measurable reductions in CRP.
