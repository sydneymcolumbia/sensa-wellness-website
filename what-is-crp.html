Inflammation 101

What is CRP?

C-reactive protein (CRP) is a protein your liver releases in response to inflammation. It is one of the most widely used markers of systemic inflammation, and tracking it over time can offer a clear picture of how your lifestyle is affecting your body from the inside.

The Basics

Understanding C-Reactive Protein

C-reactive protein is a protein produced by the liver. Under normal conditions, it circulates in the blood at very low concentrations. When the body detects tissue damage, infection, or sustained stress, immune signaling molecules called cytokines (particularly IL-6) prompt the liver to rapidly increase CRP production.

This makes CRP a reliable signal that something in the body has triggered an inflammatory response. In the short term, that response is helpful: it helps fight infections and heal injuries. The concern arises when inflammation becomes chronic, running quietly in the background for months or years without an obvious cause.

High-sensitivity CRP (hsCRP) assays are capable of detecting very small changes in CRP concentration, making them useful for tracking the kind of low-grade inflammation associated with everyday lifestyle factors like diet, sleep, stress, and physical activity.

How CRP Gets Produced

1

A stressor (infection, poor diet, poor sleep, chronic stress) activates immune cells.

2

Those cells release IL-6 and other pro-inflammatory cytokines into the bloodstream.

3

The liver detects these signals and ramps up CRP production within 4 to 6 hours.

4

CRP levels in the blood rise, providing a measurable signal of the body's inflammatory state.

Why It Matters

Why Track Your CRP?

A single CRP reading is informative. A trend over time is transformative. Regular monitoring gives you objective feedback on how your habits are affecting your body at a biological level.

01

Weight and Body Composition

Visceral fat (fat around internal organs) is one of the strongest drivers of elevated CRP. Tracking CRP alongside weight gives you a fuller picture of whether changes in body composition are actually reducing inflammation, not just moving the number on a scale.

02

Exercise and Recovery

Acute exercise transiently raises CRP, but consistent training lowers baseline inflammation over time. Monitoring CRP can help you find the balance between productive training stress and overtraining, and confirm your recovery strategies are working.

03

Energy and Sleep

Chronic fatigue and poor sleep quality are both associated with elevated CRP. Seeing your CRP fall as you prioritize sleep hygiene provides tangible confirmation that the effort is paying off beyond just feeling rested.

04

Long-Term Wellness

Chronic low-grade inflammation is a shared feature of many common conditions, including cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and accelerated aging. Keeping CRP in a healthy range is one measurable strategy for supporting long-term health.

05

Diet and Nutrition

Ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates, and excess sugar reliably raise CRP. Anti-inflammatory eating patterns consistently lower it. Your CRP can tell you whether a dietary change is working for your biology specifically, not just in population studies.

06

Stress and Mental Wellbeing

Psychological stress and social isolation both elevate inflammatory markers. Practices like consistent exercise, connection, and relaxation techniques that lower your perceived stress can also show up as measurable reductions in CRP.

Two Tests, One Protein

What Is the Difference Between hsCRP and Standard CRP?

Standard CRP and high-sensitivity CRP (hsCRP) measure the same protein, but at different sensitivities. Standard CRP assays detect levels above roughly 5 to 10 mg/L and are used to flag acute inflammation, infection, or a disease flare. High-sensitivity CRP assays detect levels as low as 0.1 to 0.3 mg/L, which is what makes them useful for tracking the low-grade, chronic inflammation tied to everyday diet, sleep, stress, and activity.

For general wellness monitoring, hsCRP is the relevant measurement. The number itself is identical chemistry; the difference is the precision of the assay reading it. Sensa is built around the high-sensitivity range because that is where lifestyle-driven changes actually show up.

Drivers of Inflammation

What Raises CRP?

CRP rises whenever the body mounts an inflammatory response. Acute triggers like infection or injury can push CRP to 20 to 100 mg/L or higher, and a single intense workout can transiently raise it two to five-fold. More relevant to everyday wellness is chronically elevated baseline CRP, which is driven by a consistent set of lifestyle factors.

  • Visceral fat. Fat around the abdominal organs is highly inflammatory. Losing 5 to 10 percent of body weight has been shown to cut CRP by about 26 percent. See obesity and inflammation.
  • Poor sleep. Restricting sleep to four hours a night for 10 days significantly raises CRP, and rotating shift workers run CRP roughly 20 percent higher than day workers. See sleep and inflammation.
  • Chronic stress. A UK Biobank analysis of more than 400,000 people linked work, financial, and life stress to higher CRP. See stress and inflammation.
  • Refined sugar and ultra-processed food. Americans average 17 teaspoons of added sugar a day, a meal-by-meal inflammatory stimulus. See sugar and inflammation and the top inflammatory foods.
  • Smoking. Current smokers carry CRP levels roughly 50 percent higher than lifelong nonsmokers. See smoking and inflammation.
  • Excess alcohol. Above about two drinks a day, CRP rises in a dose-dependent way. See alcohol and inflammation.
  • A leaky gut barrier. When the intestinal barrier becomes permeable, bacterial LPS enters the blood and drives systemic inflammation. See gut health and inflammation.
Bringing It Down

What Lowers CRP?

CRP is highly responsive to lifestyle, which is what makes it worth tracking. Because hsCRP reflects your current inflammatory state rather than a fixed trait, the same habits that reduce inflammation show up as measurable drops in your number over weeks and months.

  • Regular exercise. A meta-analysis of 83 randomized trials found regular aerobic exercise reduced resting CRP by 10 to 30 percent, and even walking 30 minutes five days a week cut CRP 16 percent over 12 weeks. See exercise and inflammation.
  • An anti-inflammatory diet. The Mediterranean pattern is the most evidence-backed, cutting cardiovascular events by about 30 percent in the PREDIMED trial while lowering CRP and IL-6. See the anti-inflammatory diet.
  • Omega-3 fatty acids. A meta-analysis of 68 trials found omega-3 supplementation lowered CRP, IL-6, and TNF-alpha, especially in people with elevated baseline inflammation. See omega-3s and inflammation.
  • Weight loss. Losing 5 to 10 percent of body weight, especially visceral fat, reduced CRP by about 26 percent in a 2019 meta-analysis. See inflammation and weight loss.
  • Intermittent fasting. A 2019 meta-analysis of 18 trials found fasting protocols reduced CRP by about 0.9 mg/L on average. See intermittent fasting and inflammation.
  • Consistent, sufficient sleep. Because poor sleep and inflammation form a feedback loop, restoring sleep is one of the most reliable ways to lower CRP. See sleep and inflammation.
  • Correcting vitamin D deficiency. Supplementation lowers CRP and IL-6 mainly in people who start deficient (below 20 ng/mL), which affects about 42 percent of American adults. See vitamin D and inflammation.

Some medications also lower CRP. Statins have well-documented CRP-lowering effects independent of their cholesterol effect, and aspirin, metformin, and omega-3 supplements lower it modestly. Any medication decision belongs with your healthcare provider.

The Biology

How Does CRP Work in the Body?

CRP is a pentameric protein produced primarily by liver cells in response to interleukin-6 (IL-6), one of the central pro-inflammatory cytokines. When IL-6 rises, whether from infection, tissue injury, or chronic low-grade inflammation, the liver ramps up CRP production within 4 to 6 hours. During an acute event, CRP levels can climb more than 1,000-fold, which is what makes it such a sensitive and fast-moving signal of the body's inflammatory state.

CRP was first described in 1930 by William Tillett and Thomas Francis, who found that blood from patients with acute pneumococcal pneumonia contained a substance that reacted with the C polysaccharide of the bacterium. The protein was named for that reaction. Decades of research since have established CRP as both a marker of inflammation and, in the case of cardiovascular disease, a signal that carries independent predictive value beyond cholesterol. See how inflammation drives heart disease and the deeper explainer on understanding your CRP numbers.

A single CRP reading is a snapshot. Because acute events like a cold, a hard workout, or a minor injury can raise CRP temporarily, the most useful signal comes from tracking the trend over time. A CRP of 8 mg/L during a cold means something very different from a CRP of 8 mg/L in a person who has been well for weeks. Repeated measurement is what separates a transient spike from a true chronic baseline.

Clinical Guidance

When Should You See a Doctor About CRP?

Consistently elevated CRP above 3 mg/L in a person who is not acutely ill, and who has no obvious lifestyle cause, is worth a conversation with a healthcare provider. A reading above 10 mg/L usually reflects an acute process such as an active infection or injury rather than chronic low-grade inflammation. CRP does not diagnose any specific condition, but it can prompt appropriate follow-up. Sensa is a general wellness tool for tracking trends, not a diagnostic device, and it does not replace professional medical evaluation.

CRP In Context

Is Testing CRP the Same as Testing for Inflammation?

CRP is the most clinically validated and widely used inflammation marker, but it is not the only one. Inflammation biomarkers are measurable substances that rise or fall with the inflammatory process, and the main options include C-reactive protein (CRP), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha).

CRP earns its central role because it responds quickly, typically within 6 to 12 hours of a change in inflammatory activity, and because it has the deepest research base linking it to long-term health outcomes. IL-6 and TNF-alpha are the upstream cytokines that trigger CRP production, so CRP effectively integrates their signal into a single, stable, easy-to-measure number. Testing Cadence

How Often Should You Test Your CRP?

Because CRP reflects your current inflammatory state rather than a fixed trait, testing regularly is more informative than a single annual reading. A traditional lab CRP test is typically done only once a year at a checkup, which captures a snapshot but misses the trend. At-home testing changes that: you can measure as often as you want to see how a specific change, a new eating pattern, a training block, or better sleep, is landing on your inflammation.

A practical approach is to establish a baseline with a few readings when you are healthy and rested, then retest every few weeks or after a meaningful lifestyle change. Avoid testing right after an intense workout, an illness, or an injury, since acute events temporarily raise CRP and can obscure your true baseline.
