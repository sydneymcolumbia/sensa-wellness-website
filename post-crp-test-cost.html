How Much Does a CRP Test Cost?

The price of a single CRP test can vary more than tenfold depending on where you get it. Understanding the range, and the hidden costs the sticker price leaves out, is the key to not overpaying.

Reviewed by the Sensa Wellness editorial team. Written to reflect current, publicly available inflammation research.

The short answer

A CRP test typically costs about $8 to $50 at direct-to-consumer and independent labs when paying cash, and roughly $75 to $250 at a hospital without insurance. High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) usually costs slightly more than standard CRP. Direct-to-consumer online labs are consistently the cheapest option, though many add a one-time blood-draw or collection fee of around $10 to $20 on top of the listed test price.

There is no single price for a CRP test. The same simple blood test can cost a few dollars or a few hundred, and the difference comes almost entirely from where you have it done and how it is billed. Because CRP is inexpensive to run in a lab, the sticker price you pay is driven far more by the venue and its overhead than by the test itself. Knowing the landscape is how you avoid paying hospital prices for a commodity test.

CRP test cost is the out-of-pocket price to measure C-reactive protein. It varies widely by venue, from single-digit dollars at direct-to-consumer labs to several hundred at hospitals, and often excludes a separate blood-draw fee.

How Much Does a CRP Test Cost by Venue?

The price of a CRP test depends primarily on where you get it. Direct-to-consumer online labs sit at the low end, independent walk-in labs in the middle, and hospital-based testing at the high end. These ranges reflect cash or self-pay prices reported across US testing platforms in 2025 and 2026 and are approximate, since prices vary by region and provider.

Standard CRP vs hs-CRP: Does the Test Type Change the Price?

High-sensitivity CRP generally costs a little more than standard CRP, though the difference is modest at most direct-to-consumer labs. The reason to care about which test you are buying is not mainly price, it is fitness for purpose. If you are tracking chronic low-grade inflammation for general wellness or cardiovascular awareness, you want hs-CRP, because it can measure the low range where meaningful differences live. Paying slightly less for a standard CRP that cannot resolve that range is a false economy. For the full distinction, see what a normal CRP level is and understanding your CRP.

The Hidden Costs the Sticker Price Leaves Out

The listed test price is rarely the whole story. Several add-ons commonly apply:

  • Blood-draw or collection fee. Many direct-to-consumer labs charge a separate one-time fee, often around $10 to $20, to draw your blood at a partner location, regardless of how many tests you order.
  • Physician order fee. Some services bundle in a small charge for the required lab order.
  • Time and travel. A clinic or lab visit costs you a trip and a wait, which is easy to overlook when comparing prices.
  • Repeat testing. Because a reliable CRP baseline requires averaging two readings about two weeks apart, and because tracking a trend means testing repeatedly, the real cost is the per-test price multiplied by how often you test.

The Cost of Tracking Over Time

Here is the part that matters most for anyone who wants to actually track inflammation rather than check it once. CRP is a marker whose value comes from repetition. Guidelines recommend two readings to set a baseline, and monitoring how your inflammation responds to lifestyle changes means testing every few weeks. At even a modest $20 to $30 per lab visit plus a draw fee each time, a year of regular tracking adds up quickly, and the logistics of repeated appointments become a bigger barrier than the money. This is exactly where the per-test model of traditional labs works against frequent monitoring. See how often you should test CRP.

The Bottom Line on CRP Test Cost

Pulling the threads together, the price of a CRP test is far less fixed than most people assume, and a little knowledge saves real money. The identical test ranges from single-digit dollars at a direct-to-consumer online lab to several hundred at a hospital, so venue is the dominant factor. High-sensitivity CRP costs slightly more than standard CRP but is the right choice for wellness tracking, making the small premium worthwhile. Sticker prices routinely omit draw fees and physician-order charges, so compare total cost rather than headline numbers. And the most overlooked point of all: because CRP earns its value through repetition, the meaningful figure for anyone tracking their inflammation is the total cost across many readings, not the price of one. Whether you need a single cheap result or an affordable way to test repeatedly determines which option is genuinely the best value for you. In short, there is no one right price, only the right price for your purpose, and knowing the difference between a one-off check and ongoing tracking is what lets you choose well instead of overpaying for the wrong model.

Is a CRP Test Covered by Insurance?

Whether insurance covers a CRP test depends on why it is ordered, and this is a common source of confusion. When a clinician orders CRP or hs-CRP to investigate a symptom or assess cardiovascular risk with a clear medical indication, it is frequently covered, though your out-of-pocket cost still depends on your deductible, copay, and whether the lab is in-network. When you order a CRP yourself through a direct-to-consumer service purely for wellness or curiosity, it is typically not billed to insurance and you pay the cash price, which as noted is often lower than an insured hospital bill anyway. This is why the cheapest path for a routine, non-urgent CRP is frequently self-pay at a direct-to-consumer lab rather than going through insurance at a hospital. Always confirm coverage and in-network status before assuming a hospital test will be inexpensive, since uninsured or out-of-network lab charges are where the largest bills appear.

Cost Versus Value: What You Actually Get

Price is only half of the equation; the other half is what the test is actually good for. A single cheap CRP tells you your inflammation level at one moment, which is genuinely useful if that is all you need. But because CRP fluctuates with recent events and its real value comes from a trend, the return on a one-off test is inherently limited. A reading of 4 mg/L means very different things depending on whether it is a stable baseline or a temporary spike from last week's cold, and one test cannot tell you which. This is why cost should be weighed against purpose. For a one-time check, optimize for the lowest reliable price. For understanding and improving your inflammation over time, the relevant cost is the total across many readings, and the venue that makes repeated testing cheap and convenient wins even if its per-test economics look different.

How to Get the Cheapest Reliable CRP Test

If you want a single CRP result at the lowest reliable price, a few steps consistently help. Order through a direct-to-consumer online lab rather than walking into a hospital, since the price gap for identical testing can exceed tenfold. Factor in the one-time draw fee, often around $10 to $20, when comparing services, because a low headline price with a high draw fee may not be the best deal. Make sure you are ordering hs-CRP if you want to track chronic low-grade inflammation, since a cheaper standard CRP cannot resolve that range. And if you anticipate testing repeatedly to follow a trend, do the full-year math rather than comparing single-test prices, because per-draw costs and repeated trips add up in ways a one-off comparison hides. That total-cost lens is exactly where an owned at-home device changes the calculation.
